Water safety is influenced by several key parameters that determine the quality and
suitability of water for human use, aquatic life, and environmental health. Tarang
AI carefully and intentionally uses these parameters to train the AI model to predict
water safety. Let's have a look at how these parameters may have an impact.


State:
The state determines the geographical location of water body and that may influence
the condition of the sample. Different states or regions may have varying levels of
industrial pollution, agricultural runoff, or natural mineral content, which directly
impact water safety.


Temprature:
Water temperature affects the solubility of oxygen, the metabolic rates of aquatic
organisms, and the growth of harmful bacteria. Higher temperatures can decrease
dissolved oxygen levels, leading to hypoxic conditions that harm aquatic life and
increase bacterial proliferation, making water unsafe.

Dissolved Oxygen (D.O.) (mg/l):
Dissolved oxygen is crucial for aquatic life. Low D.O. levels indicate pollution or excessive organic matter decomposition, which can lead to dead zones where aquatic organisms cannot survive. Water with insufficient D.O. may also promote the growth of anaerobic bacteria that produce harmful toxins. Regular monitoring of dissolved oxygen helps detect early signs of pollution and ensures water remains habitable.

Conductivity measures the water's ability to carry an electrical charge, which reflects its total dissolved solids (TDS) and ion concentration. High conductivity suggests contamination from industrial waste, sewage discharge, or salt intrusion, making water unsafe for consumption and aquatic habitats.


pH (0-14):
The pH level determines the acidity or alkalinity of water. Extreme pH levels can be corrosive, affect the bioavailability of toxins, and disrupt aquatic ecosystems. The pH level of water is determined on a scale of 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. Acidic water (low pH) can leach heavy metals, while alkaline water (high pH) can reduce nutrient solubility, both of which compromise water safety.


Fecal Coliform (MPN/100ml):
The presence of fecal coliform bacteria indicates contamination
by human or animal waste. High levels suggest the risk of
waterborne diseases like cholera, dysentery, and typhoid. Water
with elevated fecal coliform counts is considered unsafe for
drinking, recreation, or any direct human contact. Contaminated
water sources often stem from inadequate sewage treatment,
agricultural runoff, and improper waste disposal.


Biochemical Oxygen Demand (B.O.D.) (mg/l):
B.O.D. measures the amount of oxygen required to break down
organic matter in water. High B.O.D. levels indicate heavy
pollution from sewage, industrial waste, or decaying organic
material, which depletes oxygen and makes water unsuitable for
both human use and aquatic life. Excessive B.O.D. can lead to
oxygen depletion, resulting in fish kills and the disruption of
aquatic ecosystems.

Nitrate-Nitrogen (mg/l):
Excess nitrates and nitrites result from agricultural runoff, fertilizers, and sewage discharge. High concentrations can lead to eutrophication, which depletes oxygen and harms aquatic ecosystems. In drinking water, elevated nitrate levels pose serious health risks, such as methemoglobinemia ("blue baby syndrome") in infants, which reduces oxygen transport in the blood and can be fatal. Long-term exposure to high nitrate levels has also been linked to thyroid dysfunction and potential cancer risks.
